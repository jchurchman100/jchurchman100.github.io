T-Mo

Designed a new youth sports mobile app to centralize team management.

Timeframe: 3 weeks | Tools: Figma, InVision

Overview

The Problem

Youth sports can be a very busy experience. Coaches and parents must use multiple tools to manage all of their teams, which can be overwhelming at times and causes communication errors. It becomes even more difficult when parent involvement is low, leaving coaches to handle many responsibilities alone.

The Solution

T-Mo, a central team management hub that makes it simple for coaches and parents to manage and track all their youth teams. T-Mo will improve team involvement and efficiency by combining functionality that exists in tools currently at use, while making them more user-friendly and engaging.

My Role

I lead my team’s interaction design by managing our pattern library, wireframes and prototype. My goal was to keep these in line with our user personas, user flows, and usability test scenarios.

I also lead the visual design and branding for the application, creating our style guide, hi-fidelity mockups, app logo and determining the app name.

What tools are coaches currently using?

With so many possible features on the table, we decided to perform a competitive analysis on the many existing youth sports apps out there to see what we could incorporate into our design. While were able to find many useful resources the main 4 that we referred to in our design decisions were SportsEngine, GameChanger, Dick’s Team Manager and TeamSnap.

We found that while most of these applications were on both iOS and Android, most users were on iPhone with the apps also seemingly designed for iPhone. This was especially apparent in GameChanger, with 450K reviews on iPhone as opposed to 12K reviews on Android. With this in mind, we decided to design our app for Android.

What do our users need the most?

To find out what our key priorities needed to be, we sent out a user survey to a list of 20 coaches that our client provided us. We asked them how they were currently managing their teams as well as what their biggest needs and pain points were. Our survey received 11 responses and provided us some good insight on our coaches’ values.

In addition, we were able to conduct multiple user interviews with coaches and parents in person and over the phone. We also attended a sports tech event where we were able to gain more insight about our direction towards creating a true MVP.

Key Findings
  • Parents and coaches are juggling multiple tools currently.
  • Coaches use volunteer parents to help with many responsibilities.
  • Poor communication is the root of all problems.
  • It is difficult to find scorekeepers who can accurately record stats.

Define

Meet the Team

From the start we knew our main users would be coaches, but after our research we were able to narrow what they were struggling with the most as well as what they would like to see in an app. With our data compiled, I put together our primary persona.

A Coach's Journey

After defining who Coach Mike is, we wanted to visualize how he regularly proceeds through a season. So, we created a user journey map, to ideate on how things might go wrong during a season with their current management methods. In the case of Mike, because he is managing information in multiple sources that are not specifically designed for team communication, it becomes overwhelming when things begin to go wrong at a game.

Getting Parents Involved

Based on some of our research, we decided to put together a secondary persona. One of our key pain points we heard multiple times was parent involvement. We decided to pursue this further to differentiate our app and increase its market appeal. We realized that if parents also like using the app, then they are more likely to be active in their teams and more teams will adopt the app. This would be a win-win for both our user and business goals.

“Coaches use the app the most, but the team mom chooses the app.”

How will our pages function?

Our biggest challenge during this project was consolidating our features and understanding how a user would interact with such a massive app. Before we could visualize a user flow, we had to whiteboard multiple design storms to see what our main features could possibly look like so that we had a grasp on our information architecture.

During this process we came up with the idea of using a bottom navigation bar to navigate through the scheduling, messaging and payment pages of a specific team, while using a side menu to change or find teams and access global account information. The features included in the bottom nav changed multiple times throughout our process, but we narrowed it to the 3 features we felt would have the most consistent usage.

How is our data structured?

We created a rough map sitemap to help answer some of our questions about the apps information architecture:

  • Where is information being stored?
  • How is information being passed to different parts of the app?
  • What is the depth and breadth of our app features?
  • How many clicks will it take a user to accomplish a task?
  • What is the relationship between teams and leagues? Teams and members? Members and leagues?
  • What functionality can be the same between different users and pages?

Test

Usability Testing

We managed to test around 5 users with our prototype. Generally users were able to accomplish our tasks, but took a minute to adjust to the app due to the amount of content. Overall we noticed many areas our design could be improved.

Scenario
You are a youth sports coach preparing for the next game as well as scorekeeping during the game. You want to make sure everything is up to date in your team, all volunteers are accounted for, and players are paid up and ready to play.

Task List
  • A new player joined your team mid-season. Invite them to your team.
  • Assign a parent to scorekeep at the upcoming practice.
  • Send out a notification for any late payments.
  • View your current trophy point total.
  • While scorkeeping at the game, a player hit a 3 pointer. Input that stat.
Key Takewaways
  • Some confusion on CTA add buttons. Many people tapped them as a first response in tasks.
  • Team page color layout and font boldness weren't guiding focus well enough.
  • Difficulty understanding scorekeeping flow due to lack of status visibility.
  • Icons sometimes unclear without verbiage.

Iterations

Following the results of our testing, I modified the icons on our CTA buttons to increase clarity. I had to create custom icons to keep consistency between the buttons, while still indicating the specific function of each. I also modified the team page coloring and font boldness, to help guide users’ attention.

I modified our scorekeeping screens, adding a title above the court to indicate the step in the process. Colors were modified as well to relate what actions would occur when a user taps an element. Player numbers were added for visibility as well. We also made sure text was readable for button actions.

Creating a Brand

Branding Overview
As our visual design and branding lead, my overall goal for the branding was to show that while our app is about youth sports and fun, it's also efficient and usable. Because we want to encourage involvement, I named the app T-Mo, short for Team More. The name itself was inspired by a couple cartoon and video game characters. I thought it would suit our app space as a youth application. The logo was more of a trial and error situation. I made several versions, trying out different things to see what might work and ended up with the logos you see here. I tried to include the app name while keeping the logo from being too complicated.

Colors
For the colors, I wanted the app to be bright and energetic. I looked at common sports color schemes and went with blue and orange and then found good shades of each to match my aesthetic goals. I tried to keep the rest of the colors minimal and functional.

Fonts
Montserrat and Lato are both clean fonts that I’m familiar with. They’re both very readable with nice stylings of their own. Lato especially is great for reading a lot of information.

Icons
For our icons I stuck with Google Material icons for the project as they are simple and recognizable, though I did create some custom icons as well.

Next Steps

There’s a lot of room for next steps. Many areas of the application could still use some fleshing out. The main priority I see in next steps would be more usability testing. There are so many things to do in the app that it needs quite a few more rounds of testing to make sure it’s as usable as possible.

Since the app is meant to be for any type of team, it will need more templates for each sport. For the purpose of our MVP we stuck with basketball as sport to build around. However, we tried to make certain features such as scorekeeping as general as possible to help reusability and scaling of the app.

I would personally like to see the gamification feature fully built out. Defining how sponsors interact with the app to include their deals is very important and will push the app forward in terms of monetization. Also designing how users experience the trophy system as they complete tasks is interesting to me.

Conclusion

Overview
Overall, we were proud of our work. There was a lot to learn and put together within 3 weeks. While the app is far from perfect, I believe it's more than a solid start for our clients goals moving forward.

Challenges
Easily the biggest challenge we faced was figuring out an MVP. There were so many features presented to us in the beginning that we often found ourselves talking in circles. It took us almost a week of whiteboarding to understand all of the different functions our client wanted. It was also personally challenging for me since I didn't have a clue about everything that went into managing youth sports. I was able to overcome the battle against time by making quick decisions towards the end of the process. After a certain point, I decided it was time to think less and do more.

What I Learned
For one, I learned that youth sports is a logistical nightmare. This project was helpful though in terms working with a client with a lot of ideas. I had to learn that you have to say no to many things from the client side and that it's okay. I was able to successfully balance what the client wanted with what was possible for my team
